Introduction to Lockport

Locksport is a hobby where ypu learn everything that is left obscured to most people about physical security that a locking device offers you. What do i mean by a locking device? Well that is what a lock is. A lock is suppossed to deter a thief or slow them down but it wont stop them if they are determined enough. It will howvever slow them long enough so they either give up or take longer to get through the barrier you put in their way.
Lockport also teaches you how to make tools to defeat a locking device be that bypassing, picking or decoding. It educates you enough so that you can see exactly how much security the lock in question offers and takes away the delusion that some lock makers try and sell you. Locksport also has a community of lockpickers who are helpful and trade locks and tools with eachother. They also share advice and help eachother lern to pick locks. Some of these pickers can be found on social media and in forums.
Lockport is not a hobby to be used for criminal activity and only to be used on locks you own and ideally not on locks that are used or relied on as it can happen that a lock gets damaged by picking.

Warded Locks

Warded locks are old locks that rely on wards to try and obstruct a wrong key or object being inserted into the key hole first by sometimes having warded keyholes and then secondly trying to use wards inside the lock housing to prevent the object inserted from turning and unlocking the lock. The warded locks can be found with a laminated construction or on older locks pressed or rivited together. The older ones are made of Iron and some of steel like the Master lock No.22. Warded locks i think also relied on obscurity for security and are good for reliability as there are few moving parts inside to seize up. The lock can be identified by inserting a pick and you will feel like it is empty and there are no pins or anything to push on. However these locks have one or more springs loaded levers that you need to feel for and push to the side in order to free the shackle. The springs are sometime levers or wire that stop the shackle opening. If you can located the springy part inside and get past wards and push it the lock will open. Incase you don't know what a ward is, it is a piece of metal that tries to block you from pushing on the lever. One major flaw of Warded locks are skeleton keys which are keys that only have the part of the key on it that is needed to push on the lever and bypass the wards. The wrong key is blocked by wards and the right key allows the true key to pass the wards unobstructed. Locksmith i believe used to make a new key for these locks with wax impressioning. Today these locks are obsolete but you can still find them on occasion but uncommon. Old furniture locks use these sometimes.


Spring locks

Spring locks are old locks that are similar to warded lock but they have a very strong spring that can break a pick and needs a strong key to operate. The strong spring is often attached to a lever that blocks the shackle from opening.In order to pick this lock you need a strong pice of metal to push the lever and overcome the spring inside. Simple. These locks have warding on the keyhole but not inside so as locg as the tool fits it can open it. I think they were used in the USA on railroads.

Lever locks

Lever locks came along and improved the security and were considered unpickable until a tool was made to defeat them. So they had to be upgraded to have anti pick features and drill protection and more levers. These locks are seen on wooden doors and called a mortice lock. When used on exterior door they have five or more levers. They also have a anti pick notch to trap the bolt from being pushed back when being picked. The bolt has a notch that tries to catch the false notches. Some also have a curtain which is suppossed to restrice access to the levers using simple tools. These locks have different gauges of keys so not all the same key blanks fit and a small number also have a profile in the courtain for further security. These locks also have a warded keyway on uncurtained lever locks. Lever padlocks generally have no curtain and when they do they are simple single levers that need to be pushed like the previous lock. For drill protection the lock has what is called a hard plate to shiled the brass levers from drill attacks. It only slows them down. A famous maker of Lever locks is Chubb and they used to be a leader in lock security.Some lever lock are used on safes. Some lever locks have 10 levers. The more levers the longer not harder the pick is. There are some lever locks that detect if picking takes place and a hook or trap gets triggered that obstructs the keyhole. Not sure but i think it has to be reset or otherwise locks out the owner and has to be destroyed. Lever locks are defeated by impressioning, overlifting, jiggling and single lever picking. Otherwise they are destroyed. These locks are made of steel,brass and hard plate and held together with screws. The levers are spring loaded and the keys are called bit keys.The bolt is a single piece that is blocked by the levers and can sometimes have anti saw elements to resist cutting. Lever padlocks have a weakness of mostly locking on one side and being on a hinge the other side.

Pin tumbler locks

These locks are the most common type of lock and have different sub categories. They are divided into Standard pin tumblers,dimple locks,cross locks,bi locks and tubular locks. The lock consists of a lock body that is divided into the housing and plug. Both have holes drilled into them and capped of by either a plate or plugs. Inside these holes or chambers we find a pin stack which is comprised of a spring, driver pin or top pin and a keypin. The driver pin sits inside the lockhousing and plug until the key is inserted. The point of this is to block the plug from turning. The driver pins are pushed down by the springs. The keypins or bottom pins are the pins that interact with the key and vary in length which is determined by the cuts on the key. Both keypins and driver pins can be smooth standard pins or anti pick pins. Basic pin tumbler locks have only standard pins and generally five pin stacks. The most common number of pinstacks is the 5-6 pins but there are lock that have seven or more. There are also locks that claim to have a certain number but in reality it can be a lie. Pin tumbler locks are interesting because they can have a variety of key profiles that give the keyhole a particular shape. This keyway varies too in the amount of space it allows for diiferent sized keys and lockpicks and can add a challenge to picking the locks as it ideally restricts room to move picks and limits the type of keyblank that can be inserted. Pinstacks have what is called a sheer line which is the gap between the keypin and driver pin. It is also the gap between the plug and the lock housing. When the correct key is inserted the key lines up the pinstacks so that the sheer lines are all cleared and the lock can be opened.
